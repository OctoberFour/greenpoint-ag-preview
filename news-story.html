America’s Farm Families Face Economic Uncertainty

Jeff Blair

President & CEO, GreenPoint Ag · December 2, 2025 | 3-Minute Read

From labor shortages and rising input costs to trade disruptions and a delayed Farm Bill, the pressures on rural producers are mounting — and the consequences are showing up on every balance sheet across the southern U.S.

America’s food system and our farm families are navigating one of the most unpredictable economic environments in recent memory. From labor shortages and rising input costs to trade disruptions and a delayed Farm Bill, the pressures on rural producers are mounting. And the consequences are showing up in our balance sheets, putting at risk the viability of farming as both a business and a way of life.

Here in Arkansas and across the southern U.S., where agriculture is not just an industry but a way of life, farmers and ag retailer operations like the one I lead are living this uncertainty every day. Conversations that used to be about crop rotation now start with cash-flow and end with the next loan call.

The forces at play

Several pressures are compounding at once. Each one alone would be manageable. Together they are reshaping how every farm in our footprint plans the next season.

  • Input costs: Nutrient, chemistry, and seed prices remain elevated against three-year averages, even as commodity prices have softened.
  • Labor shortages: Custom-application crews, scouts, and warehouse hands are harder to find every year; H‑2A program backlogs make the spring push even tighter.
  • Trade disruption: Export demand for cotton, peanuts, and rice has been volatile, and growers are paying for that volatility in their basis.
  • A delayed Farm Bill: Without modernized commodity titles and an updated reference price, producers are budgeting against safety-net programs that were written for a very different cost structure.
We need policy that recognizes the on-the-ground reality producers face today — not the conditions of a decade ago.

What our team is doing about it

At GreenPoint Ag, we are leaning into what we do best: working shoulder-to-shoulder with growers to find the highest-return plan for every acre. That means earlier soil sampling, sharper variable-rate prescriptions, and tighter coordination on placement and timing. Small decisions, made better, compound across a season.

Our agronomists are also spending more time on financial planning conversations with our customers. Yield matters, but cost-per-acre is what determines whether a farm can plant again next year. We’re running scenarios with growers months earlier than we used to.

What comes next

Without timely intervention from Washington, the long-term picture grows steadily harder. But the ingenuity and resilience of southern producers, paired with the deep partnerships we’ve built across the region, give me confidence we can navigate this together. We have been here before. We will be here again. And the work of feeding this country, in the end, still belongs to the people who get up every morning and do it.
